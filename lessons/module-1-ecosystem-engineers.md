# Module 1: Ecosystem Engineers — How Beavers Shape Their World

**Grade Band:** 3-5
**Duration:** 3 class periods (approximately 45-50 minutes each)
**Subject Area:** Life Science, Earth Science, Computational Modeling

---

## NGSS Standards Alignment

| Standard | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| 3-LS4-3 | Construct an argument with evidence that in a particular habitat some organisms can survive well, some survive less well, and some cannot survive at all. |
| 3-LS4-4 | Make a claim about the merit of a solution to a problem caused when the environment changes and the types of plants and animals that live there may change. |
| 5-ESS2-1 | Develop a model using an example to describe ways the geosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and/or atmosphere interact. |

**Science and Engineering Practices:** Developing and Using Models, Analyzing and Interpreting Data, Constructing Explanations

**Crosscutting Concepts:** Cause and Effect, Systems and System Models

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, students will be able to:

1. Explain what a keystone species is and describe how beavers change their environment by building dams.
2. Build a computational model of a beaver dam system and predict how it changes water flow.
3. Run a simulation and collect data on how dam placement affects water distribution across a landscape.
4. Analyze model outputs to describe the relationship between beaver dams and the organisms that depend on the wetlands they create.
5. Construct an evidence-based argument about why beaver dams matter for ecosystems.

---

## Connection to Disney Pixar's *Hoppers*

In *Hoppers*, the scientists at Beaverton University are fascinated by beavers for good reason — beavers are some of nature's greatest builders. By constructing dams from sticks, mud, and stones, beavers create entirely new wetland habitats where fish, frogs, birds, and plants can thrive. In the film, when characters "hop" into robotic beavers, they experience firsthand how these animals reshape rivers and streams. In this lesson, students will do something similar — they will build a computer model of a beaver dam system and watch how it transforms a landscape, just like the real beavers (and their robotic counterparts) do in the movie.

## Lesson Procedure (5E Model)

### Day 1: Engage and Explore

#### Engage (10 minutes)

1. **Film Hook:** Show a short clip or images from *Hoppers* featuring beaver dam construction (or use publicly available beaver dam footage). Ask students: *"What do you notice about what the beavers are building? What do you think happens to the water?"*

2. **Think-Pair-Share:** Have students turn to a partner and discuss: *"If you could design any animal's home, what features would it have? Why?"*

3. **Introduce the Driving Question:** Write on the board: **"How does one animal change an entire ecosystem?"** Tell students they are going to find out by building a computer model of a beaver dam, just like scientists use models to understand nature.

4. **Vocabulary Introduction:** Briefly introduce key terms with simple definitions:
   - **Keystone species** — An animal or plant that has a huge effect on its environment compared to its numbers
   - **Ecosystem engineer** — An organism that creates, changes, or destroys a habitat
   - **Dam** — A barrier across flowing water that changes how and where the water goes
   - **Wetland** — An area where water covers the soil, creating a habitat for special plants and animals

#### Explore (30-35 minutes)

1. **Guided Model Introduction (10 minutes):** Using the projector, open the ModelIt! Beaver Dam Simulation. Walk students through the interface:
   - The landscape grid (river channel, floodplain, vegetation zones)
   - How to place a beaver dam on the river
   - The water flow indicators (arrows showing direction and volume)
   - The "Run Simulation" button and time controls
   - The data panel showing water levels at different points

2. **Paired Exploration (20-25 minutes):** Students work in pairs at their computers. Distribute the Day 1 Activity Worksheet. Students complete the following:

   **Task A — Baseline Model (No Dam):**
   - Run the simulation with no beaver dam placed
   - Record water flow direction and speed at 3 marked locations
   - Count the number of habitat zones that have enough water to support wetland organisms
   - Sketch the water flow pattern on their recording sheet

   **Task B — Single Dam Model:**
   - Place one beaver dam at the suggested location on the river
   - Run the simulation again
   - Record the same measurements at the same 3 locations
   - Sketch the new water flow pattern
   - Write one observation about what changed

3. **Quick Share:** With 5 minutes remaining, ask 2-3 pairs to share one thing they noticed changed when the dam was added.

---

### Day 2: Explore (continued) and Explain

#### Explore Continued (20 minutes)

1. **Task C — Multiple Dams Investigation:** Students continue in pairs with the Day 2 Activity Worksheet:
   - Place 2-3 beaver dams at locations of their choosing
   - Before running the simulation, **predict** what will happen to water flow and wetland area (write predictions on worksheet)
   - Run the simulation and record actual results
   - Compare predictions to results

2. **Data Collection Table:** Students fill in a data table:

   | Configuration | Water Speed (Downstream) | Wetland Area (Grid Squares) | Number of Habitat Types Supported |
   |--------------|--------------------------|----------------------------|----------------------------------|
   | No dam | | | |
   | 1 dam | | | |
   | 2-3 dams | | | |

#### Explain (25-30 minutes)

1. **Class Data Compilation (10 minutes):** Create a class chart on the board. Each pair adds their data for the three configurations. Guide students to look for patterns:
   - *"What happens to water speed downstream as we add more dams?"*
   - *"What happens to the total wetland area?"*
   - *"Did more dams always mean more habitat?"*

2. **Guided Discussion (10 minutes):** Use the compiled data to build understanding:
   - **Cause and Effect:** Beaver dams slow water down and spread it out. This creates ponds and wetlands where there were none before.
   - **Systems Thinking:** When one part of the system changes (a dam is added), it affects many other parts (water flow, plant growth, animal habitat).
   - **Keystone Species Concept:** Beavers are a keystone species because their actions create homes for many other organisms — from fish and frogs to birds and insects. Without beavers, these wetland ecosystems might not exist.

3. **Vocabulary Reinforcement (5 minutes):** Students add to their science notebooks:
   - Update definitions of keystone species and ecosystem engineer with examples from their models
   - Draw a simple diagram showing: Beaver builds dam --> Water slows and spreads --> Wetland forms --> Other animals move in

---

### Day 3: Elaborate and Evaluate

#### Elaborate (20 minutes)

1. **Design Challenge — Save the Meadow:** Present students with a scenario:
   > *"A meadow downstream is drying out because the river flows too fast and doesn't spread water to the surrounding land. Animals are leaving the area. The local wildlife agency wants to know: where should beavers build dams to restore the meadow habitat?"*

2. Students use the simulation to:
   - Test at least 2 different dam placement strategies
   - Record which configuration best restores water to the meadow zone
   - Write a brief recommendation (3-4 sentences) explaining their best dam placement and why it works, using evidence from their simulation data

#### Evaluate (25-30 minutes)

1. **Presentation (15 minutes):** Each pair presents their recommendation in 1-2 minutes. Classmates can ask one question per presentation.

2. **Individual Assessment (10-15 minutes):** Students complete the Module 1 Assessment independently:
   - Short constructed-response questions (see Assessment Rubric below)
   - Students explain how their model showed that beavers are ecosystem engineers
   - Students describe one limitation of their model (what the computer model could not show that happens in real life)

## Computational Modeling Activity — Core Details

### The ModelIt! Beaver Dam Simulation

**What students build and manipulate:**
- A grid-based landscape model with a river channel, surrounding terrain, and vegetation zones
- Students place beaver dam objects at points along the river
- The simulation calculates water flow direction, speed, and accumulation based on dam placement and terrain

**Key model variables students control:**
- Number of beaver dams (0-5)
- Dam placement location along the river
- Terrain elevation (in the Elaborate challenge, students can adjust this)

**Key model outputs students observe and record:**
- Water flow speed at measurement points
- Water coverage area (number of grid cells with water)
- Wetland habitat area (grid cells meeting water depth threshold)
- Number of organism types supported (the model assigns species to habitat types)

**Modeling concepts reinforced:**
- Models are simplified representations of real systems
- Changing one variable in a model (adding a dam) produces measurable changes in outputs
- Models help us make predictions and test ideas before trying them in the real world
- All models have limitations — they do not capture everything that happens in nature

| **Model Use and Data Collection** | Ran all simulation configurations, recorded complete and accurate data, and made thoughtful predictions before running simulations | Ran all configurations and recorded mostly complete data | Ran simulations but data recording is incomplete or contains errors | Did not complete simulation tasks or record data |

## Extension Activities

1. **Physical Model Comparison:** Using aluminum trays, clay, and craft sticks, students build a physical beaver dam model. They pour water (with food coloring for visibility) and compare results to their computer simulation. Discussion: *"How are the physical and computer models similar? Different? Which one taught you more?"*

2. **Beaver Cam Research:** Students watch real beaver dam footage from nature cameras (e.g., Explore.org live cams). They sketch a real beaver dam and label how it compares to the model they built in the simulation.

3. **Write a Letter to a Beaver:** Students write a creative letter to a beaver explaining what they learned about how important the beaver's work is — focusing on the organisms that depend on the wetlands beavers create. This reinforces the keystone species concept through a different modality.

4. **Cross-Curricular Math Connection:** Students calculate the percentage increase in wetland area between their no-dam and multi-dam configurations. They create a simple bar graph showing water coverage for each configuration.

## Teacher Notes

- **Prior Knowledge:** Students should have basic familiarity with habitats and the idea that animals need food, water, and shelter. No prior computational modeling experience is required — this module is designed as an introduction.

- **Differentiation:**
  - *For students needing support:* Pre-fill parts of the data table, provide sentence starters for the recommendation writing, and pair strategically.
  - *For advanced students:* Challenge them to add terrain modifications in the Elaborate phase, or ask them to design a dam configuration that maximizes habitat for a specific organism type.

- **Common Misconceptions:**
  - Students may think beaver dams block all water flow. Emphasize that dams slow water and redirect it — water still flows through and over dams.
  - Students may assume bigger dams are always better. The simulation will show that dam placement matters as much as dam size.

- **Hoppers Connection — Keep It Light:** The film connection is a hook to spark interest. You do not need to have seen the movie to teach this lesson. The key idea from the film is that beavers are remarkable ecosystem engineers — the same idea the science supports.

- **About the Science:** Research by ecohydrologists like Dr. Emily Fairfax (who consulted on *Hoppers*) has shown that beaver dams create fire-resistant green corridors and help with drought resilience. For grades 3-5, we focus on the simpler concept of habitat creation, but you can mention fire and drought connections if students are curious.

- **Assessment Flexibility:** The rubric is designed for formative assessment. The paired work and class data compilation are the richest assessment opportunities — circulate and listen to student conversations during simulation work.

- **Time Adjustments:** If class periods are shorter than 45 minutes, this module can be extended to 4 days by splitting the Explore phase across two sessions. The Elaborate challenge can also be assigned as a take-home activity if computer access is available.

- **Technology Troubleshooting:** Ensure the simulation loads before class begins. If internet is unreliable, the simulation can be run in offline mode (download instructions available on the ModelIt! platform). One computer per pair is ideal, but groups of 3 work as well with assigned roles (navigator, data recorder, reporter).
