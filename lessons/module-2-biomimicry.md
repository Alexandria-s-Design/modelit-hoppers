# Module 2: Biomimicry — Nature's Blueprints

**Grade Band:** 4-8
**Duration:** 3 class periods (approximately 45-55 minutes each)
**Subject Area:** Engineering Design, Life Science, Computational Modeling

---

## NGSS Standards Alignment

| Standard | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| 3-5-ETS1-1 | Define a simple design problem reflecting a need or a want that includes specified criteria for success and constraints on materials, time, or cost. |
| MS-ETS1-1 | Define the criteria and constraints of a design problem with sufficient precision to ensure a successful solution, taking into account relevant scientific principles and potential impacts on people and the natural environment. |

**Science and Engineering Practices:** Defining Problems, Developing and Using Models, Designing Solutions, Engaging in Argument from Evidence

**Crosscutting Concepts:** Structure and Function, Systems and System Models

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, students will be able to:

1. Define biomimicry and identify at least three examples of human designs inspired by animal structures or behaviors.
2. Analyze the structural and functional features of a real animal and explain how those features help it survive in its environment.
3. Use a computational design tool to build a robotic animal prototype that incorporates biomimicry principles.
4. Test their prototype in a simulated environment and iterate on the design based on performance data.
5. Present a design rationale connecting their robotic animal's features to the real animal structures that inspired them.

---

## Connection to Disney Pixar's *Hoppers*

The heart of *Hoppers* is the incredible robotic animals built by the scientists at Beaverton University. These robots are not just machines shaped like animals — they are carefully engineered to move, sense, and respond like real animals do. The robotic beaver swims and builds like a real beaver. The robotic birds sense magnetic fields for navigation. Every design choice is borrowed from nature. This is biomimicry in action — learning from millions of years of evolution to build better technology. In this lesson, students step into the role of Beaverton's engineers and design their own robotic animal prototype using a computational design tool, then test how well their nature-inspired features perform.

## Materials Needed

- Computers or tablets (1 per pair or small group) with internet access
- ModelIt! Biomimicry Design Lab (web-based modeling/design tool)
- Student design journals or worksheets (provided)
- Projector/smartboard
- "Animal Feature Cards" — printed reference cards showing 10-12 animals with key structural adaptations (e.g., gecko feet, owl wing feathers, dolphin skin, beaver tail, eagle eyes)
- Poster paper and markers for design presentations
- Optional physical prototyping supplies (for extension):
  - Cardboard, tape, pipe cleaners, straws, rubber bands
  - Recycled materials bin

## Lesson Procedure (5E Model)

### Day 1: Engage and Explore

#### Engage (15 minutes)

1. **Mystery Object Game:** Show students 3-4 images of real-world inventions alongside the animals that inspired them (do not reveal the connection yet). Examples:
   - Velcro and burdock burrs
   - Bullet train nose and kingfisher beak
   - Swimsuit material and shark skin
   - Building ventilation systems and termite mounds

   Ask: *"What do these pairs have in common?"* Let students guess before revealing the biomimicry connection.

2. **Film Connection:** Briefly describe (or show images of) the robotic animals from *Hoppers*. Explain: *"The engineers in the movie studied real animals to build robots that could move and sense the world just like those animals. This approach — copying nature's best ideas — is called biomimicry."*

3. **Driving Question:** Post on the board: **"How can we use what nature has already figured out to design technology that solves real problems?"**

#### Explore (30-35 minutes)

1. **Animal Feature Research (15 minutes):** Distribute the Animal Feature Cards to groups (3-4 students). Each group receives 2-3 cards. Students study the cards and complete a "Feature Analysis Worksheet" for each animal:

   | Animal | Key Feature | What It Does (Function) | How It Is Built (Structure) | What Human Problem Could It Solve? |
   |--------|------------|------------------------|---------------------------|-----------------------------------|
   | Gecko | Toe pads | Stick to surfaces | Millions of tiny hair-like structures (setae) | Climbing robots, adhesives |

2. **Introduction to the Design Lab (15-20 minutes):** Using the projector, demonstrate the ModelIt! Biomimicry Design Lab:
   - **Animal Library:** Browse real animals and their measured performance data (speed, grip strength, sensory range, energy efficiency)
   - **Feature Selector:** Choose structural features to incorporate into a robotic design (locomotion type, surface texture, sensory systems, body shape)
   - **Environment Simulator:** Test the design in different environments (water, forest, urban, arctic)
   - **Performance Dashboard:** View metrics like speed, energy use, stability, and task completion rate

   Students explore the tool freely for 10 minutes, browsing the animal library and trying out feature combinations.

---

### Day 2: Explore (continued) and Explain

#### Explore Continued — Design Challenge (30 minutes)

1. **Present the Design Brief:** Each group receives a design scenario. Possible scenarios (assign or let groups choose):

   - **Scenario A — Forest Fire Scout:** Design a robotic animal that can navigate a burned forest landscape to find surviving animals and water sources. It must move over uneven terrain and detect heat and moisture.
   - **Scenario B — Ocean Cleanup Collector:** Design a robotic animal that can swim through ocean water collecting small pieces of floating plastic. It must be energy efficient and able to filter debris from water.
   - **Scenario C — Urban Search and Rescue:** Design a robotic animal that can enter collapsed buildings to locate trapped people. It must climb rubble, fit through small spaces, and sense sound or body heat.
   - **Scenario D — Wetland Monitor:** Design a robotic animal that can travel through beaver-created wetlands collecting water quality data. It must handle both water and land, and carry sensor equipment.

2. **Design Process in the Modeling Tool:**

   **Step 1 — Define Criteria and Constraints (5 minutes):**
   Students write down on their worksheet:
   - What their robot must be able to do (criteria)
   - What limits their design (size, energy, environment constraints)

   **Step 2 — Select Animal Inspirations (10 minutes):**
   Using the Animal Library, students pick 2-3 real animals whose features could help solve their design problem. They record:
   - Which animal and which specific feature
   - Why they chose it (what function it provides)

   **Step 3 — Build the Prototype Model (15 minutes):**
   Students assemble their robotic animal in the Design Lab:
   - Choose a base body plan (quadruped, biped, aquatic, flying, multi-limbed)
   - Add selected biomimicry features (locomotion, sensors, surface, appendages)
   - Name their creation

#### Explain (20-25 minutes)

1. **First Test Run (10 minutes):** All groups run their prototype in the environment simulator for their assigned scenario. The Performance Dashboard shows:
   - Task completion percentage
   - Energy efficiency rating
   - Speed across terrain
   - Obstacles navigated successfully
   - Overall performance score

   Students record their results on the "Test Run Data Sheet."

2. **Class Discussion — What Worked and What Didn't (10-15 minutes):**
   - Each group gives a 1-minute summary: what they designed, what animal features they used, and how it performed.
   - Guide the discussion toward key concepts:
     - **Structure and Function:** Features work well because their structure matches the function needed. A gecko-inspired grip works for climbing because the structure (tiny setae) creates the function (adhesion).
     - **Trade-offs in Design:** No design is perfect at everything. A robot optimized for swimming may not climb well. Engineers must prioritize based on the problem.
     - **Iteration:** Real engineers (and the *Hoppers* scientists) test and redesign many times. The first version is never the final version.

---

### Day 3: Elaborate and Evaluate

#### Elaborate — Iteration Cycle (20 minutes)

1. **Redesign Based on Data:** Groups review their Day 2 performance data and identify the weakest area of their design. They:
   - Swap out or modify at least one feature
   - Explain in writing why they are making this change (referencing their test data)
   - Re-run the simulation
   - Record the new performance results alongside the original

2. **Comparison Table:**

   | Metric | Version 1 | Version 2 | Change (+/-) |
   |--------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
   | Task completion (%) | | | |
   | Energy efficiency | | | |
   | Speed | | | |
   | Obstacles cleared | | | |
   | Overall score | | | |

3. **Reflection Question:** *"Did your design improve? What does this tell you about why engineers test and iterate?"*

#### Evaluate (25-30 minutes)

1. **Design Presentation (15-20 minutes):** Each group presents their final robotic animal design to the class using a simple poster or the projected Design Lab screen. Presentations must include:
   - The environmental problem their robot solves
   - Which real animals inspired their design and why
   - Data from both test runs showing how iteration improved performance
   - One design trade-off they had to make

2. **Individual Written Assessment (10 minutes):** Students complete the Module 2 Assessment independently:
   - Define biomimicry in their own words
   - Name one animal feature from their design and explain how its structure enables its function
   - Describe why engineers use computational models to test designs before building physical prototypes
   - Identify one limitation of their model (something the simulation could not capture about real-world performance)

## Computational Modeling Activity — Core Details

### The ModelIt! Biomimicry Design Lab

**What students build:**
- A robotic animal prototype assembled from a library of animal-inspired components
- Each component has quantified performance attributes derived from real animal data (e.g., gecko setae-inspired grip rated at a specific adhesion force per unit area)

**Key model inputs students control:**
- Base body plan selection (body shape, number of limbs, size class)
- Locomotion system (e.g., beaver-style paddle tail, gecko-style climbing feet, cheetah-style sprinting legs, eagle-style wings)
- Sensory systems (e.g., owl-style low-light vision, bat-style echolocation, snake-style thermal sensing)
- Surface features (e.g., shark-style drag-reducing skin, polar bear-style insulating fur, lotus-style water-repelling coating)
- Appendages and tools (e.g., elephant-style trunk manipulator, beaver-style cutting teeth, octopus-style flexible arms)

**Key model outputs students observe:**
- Task completion rate in the assigned scenario environment
- Energy consumption per unit distance or time
- Terrain traversal success rate
- Sensory detection accuracy
- Overall performance index (weighted composite)

**Modeling concepts reinforced:**
- Computational models let us test designs quickly and cheaply before building physical prototypes
- Models use simplified mathematical representations of real physical properties
- Iteration (test, analyze, redesign, retest) is a fundamental part of engineering design
- Performance data drives evidence-based design decisions

| Criteria | Exceeds (4) | Meets (3) | Approaching (2) | Beginning (1) |
|----------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|

| **Design Process** | Defined clear criteria and constraints, selected features with strong rationale, and iterated thoughtfully based on data | Defined criteria, selected features with some rationale, and made at least one data-informed iteration | Completed a design but criteria were vague or iteration was not connected to data | Design was incomplete or not connected to the scenario |

| **Communication** | Presentation was clear, well-organized, cited specific data, and explained trade-offs thoughtfully | Presentation covered required elements and referenced data | Presentation was incomplete or did not reference data | Did not present or presentation was unclear |

## Extension Activities

1. **Physical Prototype Build:** Using cardboard, tape, pipe cleaners, and recycled materials, students build a physical model of their robotic animal. They compare what was easy to model on the computer versus what is challenging to build physically. Discussion: *"What did the computational model NOT tell you about building this?"*

2. **Biomimicry Scavenger Hunt:** Students find 5 examples of biomimicry in their school or home (e.g., hook-and-loop fasteners, aerodynamic car shapes, textured shoe soles). They photograph or sketch each example and identify the animal inspiration.

3. **Real-World Robotics Research:** Students research one real biomimicry robot (e.g., Boston Dynamics Spot, Festo BionicBee, Harvard RoboBee). They write a short comparison between the real robot and their simulated design.

4. **Cross-Curricular Writing:** Students write a narrative from the perspective of a *Hoppers*-style engineer at Beaverton University, describing the process of designing a new robotic animal. The story must include at least two real biomimicry concepts.

5. **Grade Band Differentiation Project:** Upper-grade students (6-8) can add a cost constraint to their design — each feature has a price, and they have a limited budget. This adds an additional constraint for the engineering design process.

## Teacher Notes

- **Grade Band Flexibility:** This module spans grades 4-8 intentionally. For grades 4-5, focus on 3-5-ETS1-1 and the simpler structure-function relationships. Use the provided Animal Feature Cards as the primary research source. For grades 6-8, lean into MS-ETS1-1 and MS-LS1-3, require students to do their own animal research, and expect more sophisticated design rationales.

- **Prior Knowledge:** Students should understand that animals have features that help them survive (basic adaptation concepts). No prior engineering design or computational modeling experience is required.

- **Differentiation:**
  - *For students needing support:* Reduce the number of design choices (pre-select the body plan, let students choose 1-2 features), provide sentence frames for the design rationale, and pair with a strong collaborator.
  - *For advanced students:* Add the budget constraint (extension 5), require research beyond the Animal Feature Cards, or challenge them to optimize for two competing metrics simultaneously (e.g., speed and energy efficiency).

- **Group Size:** Groups of 3-4 work well for this module. Assign roles: Lead Designer (makes final feature choices in the tool), Research Specialist (consults Animal Feature Cards and library), Data Analyst (records and compares test results), Presenter (leads the final presentation). All students should contribute to design decisions.

- **Common Misconceptions:**
  - Students may think biomimicry means copying an animal exactly. Emphasize that engineers take *principles* from nature and adapt them — a climbing robot does not look like a gecko, but its grip system works the same way.
  - Students may think the first design should be perfect. Normalize iteration — the data from the first test run is not a grade, it is information that makes the second design better.

- **Hoppers Connection — Keep It Light:** Reference the film as inspiration for the activity, but the lesson stands on its own. The idea that the *Hoppers* scientists had to study real animals before building their robots is the key pedagogical connection — students follow the same process.

- **About the Science:** Biomimicry is a well-established field. The Biomimicry Institute (biomimicry.org) maintains a database called AskNature that catalogs biological strategies. The animal performance data in the Design Lab is simplified from real published research. For teacher background, the book *Biomimicry: Innovation Inspired by Nature* by Janine Benyus is an excellent resource.

- **Assessment Flexibility:** The group presentation is the primary summative assessment. The individual written questions ensure each student can articulate key concepts independently. Adjust the balance based on your grading practices.

- **Time Adjustments:** The Day 2 design-build phase often takes longer than expected. If needed, extend to a fourth day by giving more time for iteration and reducing presentation length. Alternatively, assign the redesign iteration as a homework activity if students have computer access at home.
